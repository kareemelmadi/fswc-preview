Advanced Wound Care Dressings

What are Advanced Wound Care Dressings?

Advanced dressings use specialized materials to achieve wound bed preparation for healing and to maintain a moist wound healing environment. There are many types of advanced dressings with various functions: hydrofiber, alginates, hydroconductive, collagens, foams, gels/ointments, and many kinds of antimicrobials. In general, they are designed to be worn for longer periods of time (some up to 7 days) for fewer dressing changes and less disturbance of the wound.

What is the purpose of Advanced Wound Care Dressing Application?

  • Moist Wound Environment — Creates a moist environment ideal for cell migration and tissue growth to speed healing. Not too wet, not too dry. Moist healing also reduces pain and scarring.
  • Exudate Management — Absorbs excess wound fluid that is detrimental to wound healing and can damage the skin around the wound.
  • Debridement — Helps to break down dead tissue (necrosis). Necrosis impedes wound healing, prolongs inflammation, and is a breeding ground for bacteria.
  • Infection Prevention/Management — Some dressings contain antimicrobial agents like silver ions, iodine, organic pigments, medical-grade honey, and some are chemotactic.
  • Tissue Proliferation — Some dressings have properties that directly influence or interact with the body's cells to encourage growth.
  • Protection — Shields the wound from external trauma and contamination.

Types of Dressings and Applications

  • Foam Dressings — Typically for moderate to heavy exudate absorption. Can be used as a primary or secondary dressing.
  • Hydrocolloid Dressings — Absorb light to moderate exudate. Mostly used for partial thickness or shallow wounds. Not to be used on infected wounds.
  • Alginate/Hydrofiber/Hydroconductive Dressings — Absorb moderate to heavy exudate. The exudate causes a gelling action that keeps the dressing from sticking to the wound tissues. Often used for filling the space of deeper wounds. Not to be used for dry or lightly exudating wounds.
  • Collagen Dressings — Bioactive and attracts cell components that encourage tissue growth and reduce enzymes that cause tissue breakdown. Often used for chronic stalled wounds.
  • Antimicrobial Dressings — Infused with a variety of different antimicrobial agents to help prevent and treat wound infections.
  • Hydrogel Dressings — Donates moisture for dry wounds. May have a cooling and soothing effect on the wound.

Application Considerations

  • Tissue characteristics
  • Presence or absence of infection
  • Exudate level
  • Wound type, depth, and/or stage
  • Location on the body
  • Patient factors
  • Patient goals
  • Professional guidance

Tissue Grafts

What are Tissue Grafts?

Tissue Grafts, sometimes called "skin substitutes" or "skin grafts", are used to help the body heal a wound. They provide a boost for the body's own healing capabilities by promoting growth factors, proteins, collagen, new blood vessels, and scaffolding for tissue growth.

Types of Tissue Grafts

We use various types of grafts from donated human (allograft) and animal (xenograft) sources, and some are synthetic. Some examples are amniotic, peritoneal, fish, or tendon. Not all wounds need a graft, and there are qualifications that must be met before a graft can be considered.

Wound Debridement

What is Wound Debridement?

A procedure that removes dead, damaged, or infected tissue, or even foreign material from a wound. This is a key step to promote healing and prevent or reduce infection.

Types of Debridement

  • Sharp Debridement — Involves using sharp instruments such as a scalpel, curette, scissors, and forceps. This is performed in clinic by qualified clinicians.
  • Enzymatic Debridement — The use of an enzyme ointment that helps to break down dead tissue. This is often used in addition to other forms of debridement. The ointment is applied to the wound at home with routine dressing changes.
  • Abrasion Debridement — Uses specialized materials to scrub off thin layers of dead tissues.
  • Autolytic Debridement — The body's own ability to reduce dead tissue.

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

What is Negative Pressure Wound Therapy?

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT) is often referred to as a wound vac (vacuum assisted closure). A specialized dressing is placed into the wound and connected to a machine that provides suction. The suction promotes tissue growth and removes excess wound fluid to a collection chamber attached to the machine. NPWT is worn by the patient 24/7, and dressings are typically changed three times per week.

Benefits of NPWT

  • Promotes tissue growth to fill in deeper wounds
  • Faster decrease in wound size
  • Increases blood flow to the wound tissues
  • Fewer dressing changes (typically 3x/wk)
  • Can reduce infection risk if managed properly

Red Light Therapy

What is Red Light Therapy?

Red Light Therapy for wound healing delivers a range of visible and invisible wavelengths of red and near-infrared light (620–950nm). The light energy stimulates cells in many different ways to promote wound healing.

Benefits of Red Light Therapy

  • Stimulates collagen production
  • Increases blood flow
  • Reduces inflammation

In addition to red light, Blue and Violet light therapies are also used to reduce bacterial load.
